## PAGE POSITIONING STATEMENT

Churches are being targeted. Deliberately. By attackers who understand church operations, speak the church's language, and have proven they can exploit the trust that makes ministry possible.

In 2025, Enable Ministry Partners — one of the longest-tenured church technology firms in the country — confirmed the five most active attack types hitting churches: Business Email Compromise targeting church staff, Business Email Compromise targeting church vendors, VIP spoofing of senior pastors and executive pastors, Church Management System account compromise, and ransomware. These are not hypothetical risks. They are documented, active, and recurring.

Churches are attractive targets for three reasons attackers understand well:
1. **They carry financial assets** — weekly offering, capital campaign funds, endowments
2. **They hold sensitive data** — full congregant directories, children's ministry records, counseling notes, giving histories
3. **They are trust-based organizations** — staff and volunteers are conditioned to respond helpfully to requests from leadership, vendors, and partners

Good Shepherd Insights provides the cybersecurity leadership churches need — without selling them a product. We assess, govern, train, and protect. And we do it as the church's independent technology executive, not as a vendor with a quota.

**The market context:**
- Ransomware attacks on places of worship increased **300%+** between 2021 and 2024 (CISA Advisory)
- Global ransomware attacks rose **32% in 2025**, reaching 7,419 documented incidents (Comparitech, 2026)
- **70% of ransomware attacks** target organizations with fewer than 500 employees — the exact size profile of most churches (PreVeil, 2026)
- **64% of church leaders** believe an AI policy is important; only **5% have one** (Pushpay / Barna, State of Church Tech 2026)
- **93.5% of church leaders** are now actively engaging with or exploring AI in ministry (Church Tech Today, 2026) — creating new, ungoverned data exposure vectors
- The average data breach at a small nonprofit costs **$2.98 million** in recovery, remediation, and reputational damage (IBM, 2024)

Churches that have not made cybersecurity an intentional, documented priority are not just at risk. They are a known, exploitable target.

## ICP PAIN NARRATIVE

The Senior Pastor gets a call on a Tuesday morning. A congregant received a text message from "Pastor David" asking for a $500 gift card for a missionary emergency. The finance director just forwarded an email from the construction company — they've changed their wire transfer instructions and need the $47,000 invoice paid to a new account. The church's bookkeeper clicked a link in what looked like a DocuSign request and entered her Microsoft 365 credentials.

None of these are hypothetical. All three happened to real churches in 2025.

The pain is not that churches are unsophisticated. The pain is that they are under-resourced for the threat they face. Most churches have:
- No written security policy
- No incident response plan
- No MFA enforcement on email and ChMS accounts
- No staff training on Business Email Compromise or VIP spoofing
- No technical controls on email authentication (SPF, DKIM, DMARC)
- Vendor relationships managed with zero access governance

GSI provides the leadership layer that turns that exposure into managed, documented, trained security posture — at a cost that makes sense for a church budget.

### 2.1 — Cybersecurity Risk Assessment

**Page Title:** Church Cybersecurity Risk Assessment | Good Shepherd Insights
**Meta Description:** GSI conducts comprehensive cybersecurity risk assessments built specifically for churches — evaluating email security, ChMS access controls, network vulnerabilities, and donor data exposure.

**SEO / AEO Keywords:**

- church email security SPF DKIM DMARC

#### What It Is
A Church Cybersecurity Risk Assessment is a structured, comprehensive evaluation of a church's full security posture — identifying where donor data, member records, children's ministry information, and financial systems are exposed, and delivering a prioritized remediation plan the leadership team can act on.

This is not a generic IT security audit. Every assessment GSI conducts is built around the specific threat landscape facing churches in 2025: Business Email Compromise, VIP spoofing, ChMS account compromise, ransomware, and vendor fraud. We evaluate the exact attack surfaces these threats exploit — and we communicate findings in language that a Senior Pastor, Board Chair, or Finance Committee can understand and act on.

#### Who It's For
- Senior Pastors and Executive Pastors who suspect their church's security posture is inadequate but don't know where to start
- Finance Committees and Church Boards who have a fiduciary duty to protect donor funds and member data
- Churches that have experienced a security incident — or whose peer churches have — and need to respond proactively
- Large parishes and multi-site churches with complex technology environments and elevated data exposure

#### What GSI Does
1. **Network Vulnerability Scan** — External and internal network scan identifying open ports, unpatched services, exposed devices, and misconfigured network segments
2. **Email Security Review** — Evaluation of SPF, DKIM, and DMARC configuration — the three email authentication standards that prevent domain spoofing and VIP impersonation attacks
3. **ChMS Access Control Audit** — Review of who has access to the Church Management System, at what permission level, and whether access controls match current staff roles
4. **MFA Audit** — Verification that Multi-Factor Authentication is enforced on email (Microsoft 365 / Google Workspace), ChMS, giving platforms, and banking portals
5. **Device Inventory & Endpoint Review** — Catalog of church-owned and personal devices with access to church systems; assessment of endpoint security
6. **Third-Party Vendor Access Review** — Identification of all vendors, contractors, and MSPs with active access to church systems, and whether that access is appropriately scoped and monitored
7. **Incident Response Readiness Review** — Assessment of whether the church has a documented, practiced response plan for a security incident
8. **Prioritized Remediation Report** — A board-ready findings document with all identified risks ranked by severity, specific remediation recommendations, and a 90-day action plan

#### Outcomes
- A clear, documented understanding of the church's current security posture
- Prioritized action plan that leadership can execute — or hand to GSI for execution
- Board-ready documentation that demonstrates responsible stewardship of member data and donor funds
- Identification of the specific attack vectors most likely to be exploited given the church's current configuration

#### Why This Matters
Churches that have never conducted a formal security assessment have no documented baseline. When an incident occurs, they have no way to demonstrate they acted responsibly — to their congregation, their insurers, or their legal counsel. A risk assessment is both a protective action and a governance record.

#### Fractional CTO Transition Hook
The assessment delivers the roadmap. The Fractional CTO engagement delivers the execution — ongoing security governance, vendor oversight, staff training, and policy maintenance so the church's security posture continues to improve rather than eroding over time.

### 2.2 — Security Policy Development

**Page Title:** Church Security Policy Development | Good Shepherd Insights
**Meta Description:** GSI builds formal cybersecurity policies for churches — acceptable use, password and MFA policy, data classification, incident response, and board governance frameworks.

**SEO / AEO Keywords:**
- church cybersecurity policy
- church IT acceptable use policy
- church data security policy
- church incident response plan
- church password policy
- church BYOD policy
- church security governance
- cybersecurity policy for nonprofits

#### What It Is
Security Policy Development is the documented governance framework that tells every staff member, volunteer, and vendor how to handle church data, systems, and devices — and what to do when something goes wrong.

Most churches have no written security policies. When a staff member asks "should I click this link?", "can I use my personal phone for ChMS access?", or "what do I do if I think our email was hacked?" — there is no documented answer. That gap is not just a security risk. It is a governance failure that exposes the church to liability, reputational harm, and the full cost of an unmanaged incident.

GSI builds policy frameworks that are practical, church-specific, and written in language that staff and volunteers can actually understand and follow.

#### Who It's For
- Church Boards and Finance Committees that recognize their governance responsibility extends to data and technology
- Senior Pastors preparing for an insurance audit, denominational review, or compliance requirement
- Executive Pastors building a formal operations infrastructure for a growing church
- Churches that have experienced a security incident and need to demonstrate corrective action

#### What GSI Builds
1. **Acceptable Use Policy (AUP)** — Rules governing staff and volunteer use of church-owned and personal devices, software, email, and internet access
2. **Password & MFA Policy** — Minimum password requirements, MFA enforcement standards, and password manager guidance
3. **Data Classification Policy** — Framework for identifying which data is public, internal, confidential, or restricted — and how each category must be handled
4. **Vendor Access Policy** — Rules governing how third-party vendors access church systems: provisioning, scope limitations, monitoring, and deprovisioning
5. **Incident Response Plan** — Step-by-step documented procedure for responding to a security incident: detection, containment, notification, remediation, and post-incident review
6. **BYOD Policy** — Rules governing personal device use for church system access, including minimum security requirements and remote wipe authorization
7. **Email & Social Media Security Guidelines** — Staff guidance on email hygiene, link verification, wire transfer verification protocols, and social media account security
8. **Board Technology Governance Framework** — Board-level policy establishing oversight responsibility for technology and cybersecurity risk

#### Outcomes
- A complete, documented security policy library that can be presented to the church board, insurers, and denominational leadership
- Clear, actionable guidance for every staff member and volunteer on their security responsibilities
- A documented incident response plan that reduces chaos — and liability — when something goes wrong
- A governance foundation for ongoing security leadership under the Fractional CTO engagement

#### Fractional CTO Transition Hook
Policies require maintenance. As the threat landscape evolves, staff changes occur, and new technologies are adopted, the policy library needs to be updated. GSI's Fractional CTO engagement provides the ongoing governance oversight to keep the church's security policies current and enforceable.

### 2.3 — Staff & Volunteer Security Training

**Page Title:** Church Cybersecurity Training for Staff & Volunteers | Good Shepherd Insights

#### What It Is
Staff & Volunteer Security Training is church-specific, non-technical awareness training designed to turn the church's people — historically the most exploited attack surface — into the first line of defense.

Attackers targeting churches have studied church culture. They know that staff are trained to be helpful and responsive. They know that executive pastor impersonation requests for gift cards work because staff want to serve their leaders. They know that vendor email compromise works because churches trust their long-term partners. Generic cybersecurity training doesn't address any of this. GSI's training is built around the exact scenarios attacking churches in 2025 — using real examples, church-specific language, and practical verification habits that staff can apply immediately.

#### Who It's For
- All church staff — regardless of technical background
- Volunteers with ChMS, giving platform, or financial system access
- Children's ministry volunteers and staff who handle sensitive family and minor data
- Finance staff and bookkeepers who process transactions and vendor payments
- Church administrators who manage vendor relationships and wire transfers

#### What GSI Delivers
1. **Business Email Compromise (BEC) Recognition** — How to identify when an email account has been compromised, what the warning signs look like, and what the verification protocol is before acting on any financial request
2. **VIP Spoofing Awareness** — How pastor impersonation attacks work, how to verify identity through a secondary channel, and what gift card requests from "leadership" should trigger
3. **Phishing Identification** — Practical skills for evaluating links, attachments, and sender addresses — including the sophisticated spear-phishing attacks targeting church staff by name and role
4. **Safe Donor Data Handling** — How to access, store, transmit, and dispose of congregant data — including giving records, children's ministry information, and counseling-related data
5. **Password Hygiene & MFA Usage** — Practical password management and how to use MFA correctly on every church system
6. **Social Engineering Awareness** — How to recognize manipulation attempts — by phone, text, email, and in-person — that target church staff's helpful nature
7. **ChMS Access Hygiene** — Best practices for ChMS login security, shared account risks, and what to do when a staff member with ChMS access leaves the church
8. **Children's Ministry Data Protection** — Specific training for children's ministry staff and volunteers on handling minor data, photo release compliance, and check-in system security

#### Delivery Format
- Live workshop (in-person or virtual) with role-specific breakout sessions
- Written quick-reference guides for each attack scenario
- Annual refresher training cadence

#### Outcomes
- Staff and volunteers who can recognize the exact attack types targeting churches in 2025
- A documented training record demonstrating the church's commitment to security awareness
- Measurable reduction in the human-factor risk that accounts for the majority of successful church cyberattacks

#### Fractional CTO Transition Hook
Security awareness training is not a one-time event — it's an ongoing program. New staff join, the threat landscape evolves, and training needs to refresh annually. GSI's Fractional CTO engagement maintains the training program as a permanent operational component of the church's security posture.

### 2.5 — Data Privacy & Compliance

**Page Title:** Church Data Privacy & Compliance | Good Shepherd Insights
**Meta Description:** GSI helps churches build data privacy frameworks — covering COPPA compliance for children's programs, PCI-DSS for giving platforms, data retention policies, and breach notification readiness.

**SEO / AEO Keywords:**
- church data privacy compliance
- COPPA compliance for churches
- church PCI DSS compliance
- church data retention policy
- church member data privacy
- children's ministry data compliance
- church privacy policy
- church data breach response

#### What It Is
Data Privacy & Compliance is a structured engagement to inventory the personal data a church collects, assess compliance with applicable legal and regulatory frameworks, establish data handling policies, and build breach notification readiness.

Churches collect more personal data than most organizations their size: member and family records, giving histories, children's ministry enrollment data (including minors), counseling notes, background check results, and financial information. Most of that data is held in systems that were not selected with data privacy in mind — and managed by staff who have received no formal data privacy training.

The regulatory landscape is real: COPPA governs the collection of data from minors, PCI-DSS governs the handling of credit card and payment data, and state-level privacy laws are expanding rapidly. For churches, non-compliance is not just a legal risk — it is a congregational trust issue.

#### Who It's For
- Churches running children's programs, schools, or youth ministries that collect data on minors
- Churches using online giving platforms that process credit and debit card transactions
- Church Boards and Finance Committees responsible for data governance
- Large parishes with complex data environments: schools, counseling ministries, food banks, and community programs
- Churches that have experienced a data incident and need to build documented compliance posture

#### What GSI Does
1. **Data Inventory** — Comprehensive mapping of every category of personal data the church collects, where it is stored, who has access, and how long it is retained
2. **COPPA Review** — Assessment of children's ministry data practices against COPPA requirements: what data is collected on minors, how parental consent is obtained and documented, and how that data is stored and shared
3. **PCI-DSS Review** — Assessment of the church's giving platform and donation processing practices against PCI-DSS standards: cardholder data storage, transmission security, and vendor compliance
4. **Privacy Notice Development** — Drafting of a church privacy notice that accurately describes data collection and handling practices and meets legal disclosure requirements
5. **Data Retention & Deletion Policy** — Documented schedules for how long each category of data is retained, when it is archived, and how it is securely deleted
6. **Third-Party Data Sharing Review** — Audit of every vendor, platform, and partner that receives church data — and whether data sharing agreements are appropriate and documented
7. **Breach Notification Readiness** — A documented protocol for responding to a data breach: internal notification chain, regulatory notification requirements, and congregational communication plan

#### Outcomes
- A documented data inventory that forms the foundation of all privacy governance
- Compliance posture against COPPA and PCI-DSS with identified gaps and remediation plan
- A written privacy policy and data retention schedule
- Breach notification readiness — so if an incident occurs, the church responds correctly, quickly, and in compliance with applicable law

#### Fractional CTO Transition Hook
Data privacy compliance is not a one-time project — it is an ongoing governance practice. As the church adds new tools, expands programs, and changes vendors, the data inventory and compliance posture need to stay current. GSI's Fractional CTO engagement provides the sustained leadership to maintain privacy governance as a permanent operational function.
