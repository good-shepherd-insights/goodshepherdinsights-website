# GSI Brand Voice

## Positioning

Good Shepherd Insights is a Fractional CTO for churches. We provide strategic technology leadership — vendor management, security, ChMS optimization, data integration, and operational oversight — without the cost of a full-time executive. No competitor owns this category. Every line of copy must reinforce this position or be cut.

**Why we exist:** Churches make technology decisions in a vacuum. Vendor pitches drive purchases. Staff enter the same data by hand across 5–8 disconnected tools. MSP contracts run 40–60% above market rate. We replace that vacuum with executive-grade oversight.

**Audience:** Senior pastors, executive pastors, church business administrators, and board chairs at East Coast churches with 200–2,000 attendance and $200K–$5M budgets. They are not technical buyers. They are responsible buyers who lack technical guidance.

## Voice Traits

1. **Executive, not casual.** Speak to church leaders as professional peers. No exclamation marks. No "we're here to help!" energy. Use the register of a CTO briefing a board.
2. **Specific, not vague.** Every claim needs a number or a named source. If you cannot cite it, do not write it. "Churches overpay MSPs 40–60%" (industry estimate) is on-voice. "Churches waste money on tech" is off-voice.
3. **Mechanism-focused, not metaphor-heavy.** Describe how the service works. Name the inputs, the process, the outputs. Never describe how a problem "feels" using figurative language.
4. **Ministry-aware, not ministry-cheesy.** Respect that the audience's context is ministry. Use "mission," "staff," and "ministry" when they are the accurate words. Never use "Kingdom impact," "shepherd your flock," or similar church jargon as marketing devices.

## Tone by Context

| Context | Target emotional shift | How to achieve it |
|---|---|---|
| Hero | Confusion → Clarity | Name the category. Name the problem. Name the solution. One sentence each. |
| Pain / About | Overwhelm → Recognition | Describe the problem with data, not drama. Mirror the buyer's exact vocabulary from the ICP. |
| Services | Skepticism → Understanding | List what we do, how we do it, what the output is. Professional scope, not promises. |
| Stats / Proof | Doubt → Authority | Source every number. Label the source. No inflated or unverifiable claims. |
| CTA | Inertia → Action | Offer a concrete deliverable with a clear outcome. Never "let's chat" or "start your journey." |
| Support / Apology | Frustration → Relief | Short sentences. No new jargon. Acknowledge the problem directly, state the fix. |

## Style Rules

- Always cite the source when using a statistic. Example: "300%+ increase in ransomware attacks on places of worship (CISA Advisory, 2021–2024)."
- Never use metaphors, similes, or figurative language for pain points. "Disconnected tools" is literal and on-voice. "Tech chaos" is figurative and off-voice.
- Never use superlatives ("best," "leading," "premier," "trusted") without verifiable proof.
- Never stack adjectives. "Strategic technology leadership" is on-voice. "Innovative, world-class, cutting-edge strategic technology leadership" is off-voice.
- Always use the buyer's vocabulary. The ICP documents exact phrases: "vendor pitches," "ad-hoc decisions," "manual data entry," "double data entry." Use these verbatim.
- Never invent terms. If a word does not appear in the ICP, the competitive research, or established industry usage, do not introduce it.
- Always name the deliverable in CTAs. "Request Your Technology Strategy Audit" is on-voice. "Get started" or "Contact us" is off-voice.
- Never use "we" as a vague authority. Tie every "we" statement to a specific role or action: "We function as your Fractional CTO," "We audit your vendor contracts."
- Use short sentences for urgency. Use medium sentences for explanation. Never use sentences longer than 25 words in UI-facing copy.

## Terminology

### Required terms (use these, not alternatives)

| Use | Not |
|---|---|
| Fractional CTO | virtual CTO, part-time CTO, tech consultant |
| Technology Strategy Audit | free consultation, discovery call, needs assessment |
| Executive oversight | tech support, IT help, managed services |
| Strategic roadmap | technology plan, tech blueprint, digital strategy |
| Ad-hoc technology decisions | tech chaos, tech mess, technology nightmare |
| Vendor selection and contract management | vendor management, vendor relations |
| ChMS optimization | church software help, ChMS support |

### Banned phrases

- "Hope and a prayer" → write "no technology strategy"
- "Tech chaos" → write "ad-hoc technology decisions"
- "Break-fix" alone → always contrast: "reactive break-fix cycle" vs. "proactive strategic roadmap"
- "Tech guy" → write "volunteer technology support"
- "Journey" (as a customer-experience noun) → write "process," "engagement," or "roadmap"
- "Transform" / "transformation" → write "replace," "transition," or "optimize"
- "Partner" (vague) → write "Fractional CTO" or "strategic technology leadership"

## On-Voice / Off-Voice Examples

**ON:** "Replace reactive tech fixes with a professional strategy."
**OFF:** "Stop the tech chaos and take control of your church's technology."

**ON:** "Churches overpay MSPs 40–60% above market rate (industry estimate)."
**OFF:** "Churches are getting ripped off by their IT vendors."

**ON:** "We function as your Fractional CTO — executive oversight for vendor management, data security, and operations."
**OFF:** "We're your trusted technology partner, guiding you through the digital landscape."

**ON:** "Get a Technology Strategy Audit: a professional assessment of your current stack and a roadmap forward."
**OFF:** "Let's chat about how we can help transform your church's technology."

**ON:** "Your staff enters the same data across 5–8 disconnected tools. We fix that."
**OFF:** "Say goodbye to data silos and hello to seamless integration."

## Emotional Targets

| Emotion to remove | Banned copy pattern | Replacement |
|---|---|---|
| Anxiety | Fear-based urgency ("Don't let...", "Before it's too late...") | Factual urgency with cited data |
| Confusion | Vague promises and jargon | Specific deliverables and outcomes |
| Overwhelm | Long paragraphs, stacked features | Short sentences, one idea per line |
| Skepticism | Unverified superlatives | Sourced statistics and named competitors |
