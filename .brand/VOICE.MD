# Good Shepherd Insights — Brand Voice

## Positioning

Good Shepherd Insights is a partnership and guidance firm for churches navigating technology decisions. We are not an IT service, not a church technology vendor, and not a replacement for existing providers. We are the knowledgeable partner that helps churches choose the correct MSPs and technology for their specific situation — providing insightful decision-making focused on direction and mission.

**Core purchase:** Not a service — Partnership and Guidance that puts the church on the right path.

## Voice Traits

- **Clear** — Say what you mean in plain language. No jargon without purpose, no filler words, no vague claims.
- **Knowledgeable** — Demonstrate understanding of the landscape: MSPs, ChMS platforms, security tools, vendor contracts. Speak as someone who has seen what's out there.
- **Understanding** — Acknowledge that churches already have tools, vendors, and people they work with. Never dismiss what's in place. The role is to guide better choices, not to tear down.
- **Directed** — Every piece of copy points toward a decision or an outcome. No content for content's sake.

## Tone by Context

| Context | Tone | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Homepage / Hero | Confident and direct. Name what churches struggle with without blaming them. | "Disconnected tools are draining your staff's time." |
| Services | Knowledgeable and guiding. Show expertise without condescension. | "We help you evaluate vendors, manage contracts, and choose the right technology for your church." |
| Pain points | Empathetic and specific. Mirror their reality using concrete details. | "5-8 disconnected tools. ChMS features used at under 40%." |
| CTA / Conversion | Professional and inviting. Offer a concrete deliverable. | "Get a Technology Strategy Audit." |
| Support / Apology | Honest and brief. No spin. | "We missed that. Here's what we'll do." |
| Teaching / Docs | Patient, structured, no assumptions about technical skill. | Use short sentences. Define terms on first use. |

## Style Rules

- Always write in active voice.
- Never attack competitors, "the old way," or existing vendors. GSI understands what's out there and helps churches choose better — not replace everything.
- Never use fear-based urgency ("Don't let hackers destroy your church") or hypey vagueness ("transform your church's digital future").
- Never position GSI as a replacement for MSPs, ChMS platforms, or existing IT support. GSI guides the choice of those partners.
- Use specific data over adjectives. Write "5-8 disconnected tools" not "a mess of tools."
- Keep sentences under 20 words in headlines and CTAs.
- Remove filler words: "actually," "really," "simply," "just," "that" (when unnecessary).
- Capitalize "Partnership" and "Guidance" only when used as defined GSI terms (the core offering). Otherwise lowercase.

## Required Terminology

| Use | Not |
|---|---|
| Partnership and Guidance | managed services, IT support |
| Technology Strategy Audit | free consultation, discovery call |
| Fractional CTO | outsourced IT, tech support |
| technology leadership | tech management |
| vendor selection / contract management | vendor management |
| choose the right MSP/tech | replace your vendor, fire your IT |
| strategic roadmap | technology plan (too generic) |
| disconnected tools | tech chaos, tech mess |

## Banned Language

- "Replace reactive tech fixes" — attacks the old way
- "Break-fix cycle" — implies contempt for current arrangements
- "Stop relying on vendor pitches" — adversarial toward existing relationships
- "Tech chaos," "tech mess," "tech nightmare" — fear-based and vague
- "Transform," "revolutionize," "disrupt" — hypey
- "Hope and a prayer" — dismissive of church culture
- Any construction that positions GSI as replacing existing vendors or IT staff

## Emotional Targets

| Touchpoint | Target Feeling | How to Achieve It |
|---|---|---|
| Homepage | "Someone finally understands our situation." | Use specific, recognizable details from their daily reality. No abstraction. |
| Services | "This is expertise I can trust." | Name the landscape. Reference real vendor categories. Show breadth of knowledge. |
| Stats / Proof | "The numbers are real, not inflated." | Cite sources. Use conservative ranges. Label estimates clearly. |
| CTA | "This is a concrete next step, not a commitment." | Offer a named deliverable (Technology Strategy Audit). Keep language professional, not salesy. |
| Onboarding | "We're in good hands." | Short sentences. No new jargon in CTAs. Clear expectations. |

## On-Voice Examples

- "We help you choose the right technology partners and tools for your church's mission." (Partnership positioning, not replacement.)
- "Most churches work with 5-8 disconnected tools. We bring clarity to what you have and where to go next." (Specific data, understanding tone, directed outcome.)

## Off-Voice Examples

- "Stop relying on vendor pitches and ad-hoc decisions." (Attacks existing relationships. Off-voice.)
- "Replace your break-fix cycle with strategic leadership." ("Break-fix" is contemptuous of current arrangements. Off-voice.)
- "We'll transform your church's technology." (Hypey. Off-voice.)
