# VOICE.MD — Good Shepherd Insights Brand Voice

All agents must read this file before writing copy. Rules here override defaults.

## Positioning

Good Shepherd Insights is a **Partnership and Guidance** firm for churches navigating technology decisions. We are not a replacement for existing vendors or IT staff — we are a knowledgeable partner that helps churches choose the right MSPs, software, and technology for their mission.

**Category:** Fractional CTO for Churches
**Audience:** Senior pastors, executive pastors, and church business administrators at mid-sized churches (200–2,000 attendance) on the US East Coast.
**Problem we solve:** Disconnected and scattered technology processes — churches making ad-hoc vendor decisions without strategic guidance, leading to wasted budget, underutilized tools, and staff frustration.
**Why GSI, not competitors:** Competitors sell software or one-time audits. GSI provides ongoing strategic partnership — guiding vendor selection, securing data, and building a roadmap — so the church makes better decisions, not just different ones.

## Voice Traits

1. **Clear** — Short sentences. Plain words. No jargon without definition.
2. **Knowledgeable** — Cite specific data. Name sources. Never generalize.
3. **Understanding** — Acknowledge the complexity churches face. Never mock or blame.
4. **Direct** — Say what the problem is and what GSI does about it. No hedging.

## Style Rules

- Always write in second person ("you," "your church"). Never third-person abstractions.
- Always cite data with a source. Never use unsourced claims.
- Always use specific numbers over vague language ("5–8 disconnected tools," not "many tools").
- Never use filler words: "actually," "really," "simply," "just," "that said."
- Never use exclamation marks outside of CTA buttons.
- Never use scare language, urgency tactics, or fear-based framing.
- Never attack existing vendors, IT staff, or the church's current approach. Frame everything as guidance, not replacement.
- Never use "break-fix," "chaos," "mess," "disaster," or similar contemptuous terms.
- Capitalize Fractional CTO, ChMS, and Technology Strategy Audit as proper nouns.
- Use sentence case for all headings and labels. Never title-case a sentence.
- Keep stat labels under 5 words for mobile display.

## Service Names

The following 4 service names are canonical. Use them exactly as written — no abbreviations, no rewording, no alternate names.

1. **Church Technology Strategy** — No tech direction. Entry point via Technology Strategy Audit. Includes data protection and security guidance.
2. **ChMS Selection & Management** — ChMS chaos, under-40% utilization.
3. **Church Vendor Selection & Review** — Vendor confusion and overcharge. Advocacy for the church.
4. **Church Systems Integration** — Data silos, manual entry, disconnected website. Includes website design and development.

Rules:
- Always lead with "Church" — it is how the market searches.
- Never use corporate consulting language ("Advisory," "Optimization," "Management" outside ChMS context).
- Service 1 (Church Technology Strategy) includes data protection and security guidance — never list data protection or cybersecurity as a separate service.
- Service 4 (Church Systems Integration) includes website design and development — never list website as a separate service.
- Name the outcome, not the process. A pastor should understand the service name on first read.
- Every service description must use second person, cite specific data, and connect the technology problem to a ministry outcome.

## On-Voice Examples

> "We guide you past vendor confusion with the knowledge to choose the right technology and partners for your church."
> — Hero description. Direct, second person, no filler.

> "With an average of 5–8 disconnected tools and ChMS utilization often under 40%, church staff spend more time on manual data entry than on ministry."
> — Problem section. Specific, sourced, empathetic.

> "Get a Technology Strategy Audit — a professional assessment of your current tech stack and a strategic roadmap forward."
> — CTA. Professional, clear next step, no pressure.

## Off-Voice Examples

> ~~"Stop relying on vendor pitches and ad-hoc decisions."~~ — Attacks current approach. Off-voice.
> ~~"Replace reactive tech fixes with a professional strategy."~~ — "Replace" implies the old way is wrong. Off-voice.
> ~~"We move you from a break-fix cycle to a proactive roadmap."~~ — "Break-fix" is contemptuous. Off-voice.
> ~~"Stop the cycle of expensive mistakes."~~ — Blaming tone, fear-based. Off-voice.
> ~~"Ensure your tools actually support your mission."~~ — "Actually" is filler. Off-voice.
